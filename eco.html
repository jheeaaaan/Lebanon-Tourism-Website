Lebanese Tourism — Nature Series

Eco-Tourism
in Lebanon

Lebanon is a country of extraordinary natural contrasts, cedar forests, alpine peaks, river gorges, and Mediterranean turtle-nesting beaches. All within a country smaller than Connecticut.

01 — Introduction

Nature as Destination

Lebanon's natural landscape is one of the most compressed and varied in the world. In the span of a single hour's drive, a traveler can descend from snow-covered alpine peaks to a warm Mediterranean coastline, passing through cedar forests, river valleys, terraced vineyards, and wildflower meadows along the way.

Despite its small size, Lebanon hosts over 9,000 plant species, more per square kilometer than almost anywhere else in the Middle East. It sits at the intersection of three biogeographic zones, giving it an ecological richness far beyond its area.

Eco-tourism here means engaging with a natural world that is genuinely fragile. Lebanon has lost much of its forest cover, its rivers face pollution, and its coastline is under development pressure. Choosing responsible eco-tourism, supporting protected areas, local guides, and conservation efforts, is one of the most meaningful things a visitor can do.

From the UNESCO cedar groves of the north to the turtle nesting beaches of the south, Lebanon's natural world rewards those who slow down and travel with care.

02 — Nature Reserves & Protected Areas

Lebanon's Protected Wild Places

Lebanon has established a network of nature reserves and protected areas that shelter some of its most significant ecosystems, from ancient cedar forests to coastal wetlands. These reserves are the backbone of the country's eco-tourism offer and the front line of its conservation efforts.
UNESCO Biosphere Reserve
Chouf Cedar Reserve
📍 Chouf, Mount Lebanon

Lebanon's largest nature reserve, covering 550 km² of cedar forests, wolves, golden eagles, and over 250 bird species. A network of hiking trails winds through the reserve with some of the finest views in the country.

Coastal Nature Reserve
Tyre Coast Nature Reserve
📍 Tyre, South Lebanon

Protects a stretch of natural sandy coastline that serves as a critical nesting ground for endangered loggerhead and green sea turtles. Guided nighttime turtle-watching walks (June to September) are one of Lebanon's most unforgettable eco-experiences.

Marine Reserve
Palm Islands Nature Reserve
📍 Tripoli, North Lebanon

Lebanon's only marine reserve and a UNESCO Ramsar wetland. The islands host sea turtles, monk seals, and migratory birds. Clear shallow waters make it excellent for snorkeling, with boat trips available from Tripoli in summer.

Forest Reserve
Horsh Ehden Forest Reserve
📍 Ehden, North Lebanon

A dense mountain forest above Ehden sheltering rare plant species found nowhere else on Earth, cedar, fir, juniper, and wild pear. Entry requires a permit, which helps preserve its remarkable ecological integrity.

Ramsar Wetland
Ammiq Wetland
📍 Beqaa Valley

Lebanon's largest freshwater wetland and one of the most important bird habitats in the eastern Mediterranean. Over 260 species recorded here, including flamingos, white pelicans, and migratory raptors, a paradise for birdwatchers.

UNESCO World Heritage
Cedars of God - Bsharri
📍 Bsharri, North Lebanon

An ancient cedar grove near Bsharri with trees over 1,000 years old. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, carefully protected and managed. Walking among trees that predate the Crusades is a quietly humbling experience.

03 — Natural Landscapes & Scenery

The Wild Face of Lebanon

Beyond its protected reserves, Lebanon's wider natural landscape is filled with gorges, rivers, waterfalls, alpine meadows, and coastlines that together make it one of the most scenically varied countries in the Middle East.
01
UNESCO World Heritage - North Lebanon
Qadisha Valley

Lebanon's most dramatic landscape, a deep limestone gorge with walls rising over 1,000 meters, sheltering ancient monasteries, hermit caves, and a mountain river. The valley floor trail is one of Lebanon's finest hikes.

02
River Gorge - Keserwan-Jbeil
Nahr Ibrahim, Adonis River

The ancient Adonis River flows from its sacred spring at Afqa, where the river emerges from a cave at the base of a towering cliff, down through a dramatic gorge to the sea near Byblos. One of Lebanon's most beautiful and historically resonant walks.

03
Wild Forest - Akkar, North Lebanon
Akkar Highland Forests

Lebanon's most ecologically pristine region, dense cedar, pine, and oak forests cut through by rushing rivers. Largely undeveloped and rarely visited, with seasonal waterfalls and hidden valleys that feel entirely untouched.

04
Alpine Plateau - Beqaa
Yammouneh Lake & Highlands

A serene highland plateau above 1,500 meters, centered on a sacred mountain lake surrounded by wildflower meadows. Extraordinary silence, rare birdlife, and panoramic views across the Beqaa Valley to the Anti-Lebanon mountains.

04 — Activities in Eco-Tourism

How to Experience Lebanon's Nature

Lebanon's compact geography means an extraordinary range of outdoor activities is available within a small area. From mountain hiking to sea kayaking, the country offers nature experiences for every level of fitness and interest.

Hiking & Trekking
The Lebanon Mountain Trail stretches 440 km from north to south, passing 75+ villages, forests, and mountain scenery. It can be explored in sections or as a full multi-week journey.

Birdwatching
Lebanon lies on a major migration route. Ammiq Wetland, Palm Islands, and the Chouf Reserve are prime sites, especially during spring and autumn migrations.

Skiing & Snow Sports
Four ski resorts operate from December to March at altitudes up to 2,800 meters, making it possible to ski in the morning and swim in the sea later the same day.

Mountain Biking
The forests and mountain roads of the Chouf, Keserwan, and northern Lebanon offer excellent biking routes through cedar forests and alpine villages.

Sea Turtle Conservation
The Tyre Coast Reserve organizes guided turtle walks during nesting season (June–September), allowing visitors to observe endangered turtles nesting on the beach.

Diving & Snorkeling
The Palm Islands and underwater ruins at Tyre offer the rare chance to snorkel among Roman columns, anchors, and amphora from an ancient city.

04.5 — Must-Visit Eco Spots

Places You Cannot Miss

From ancient caves to sacred forests, these are Lebanon's most iconic natural destinations, each one a reminder of just how extraordinary this small country's natural world truly is.

BEIRUT

Lebanon's vibrant capital, known for its seaside Corniche, museums, nightlife, and mix of historic and modern architecture.

BYBLOS (JBEIL)

One of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities, with an ancient port, Crusader castle, and charming coastal old town.

BAALBEK

Home to the largest and best-preserved Roman temples in the world. A UNESCO World Heritage Site and a must for history lovers.

💕 Fun FAQs

  1. Did you know Lebanon has one of the oldest cities in the world?

    2. Byblos (Jbeil) is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on Earth, with history spanning over 7,000 years.

  2. What is Lebanon's nickname?

    3. Lebanon is often called the "Switzerland of the Middle East" because of its mountains, ski resorts, and natural beauty.

  3. Why is the cedar tree important to Lebanon?

    4. The cedar tree is a national symbol of Lebanon and appears on the Lebanese flag.

JEITA GROTTO

A spectacular system of limestone caves filled with stalactites and stalagmites, Lebanon's most popular natural attraction.

CEDARS OF GOD

A UNESCO grove of ancient cedars near Bsharri, some over 1,000 years old. Lebanon's most sacred and moving natural site.

TRIPOLI

Lebanon's second city, with one of the Arab world's best-preserved medieval old cities, Mamluk souks, hammams, and a Crusader citadel.

  1. Can you really ski and swim on the same day in Lebanon?

    2. Yes! Due to Lebanon's unique geography, visitors can ski in the mountains and swim in the Mediterranean on the same day.

  2. What languages are spoken in Lebanon?

    3. Lebanon is multilingual. Arabic is the official language, but many also speak French and English, especially in cities.

  3. Is Lebanese food spicy?

    4. Lebanese cuisine is more flavorful than spicy, focused on fresh herbs, olive oil, and lemon rather than heat.

SIDON (SAIDA)

A historic coastal city famous for its Sea Castle, old markets, and Phoenician roots. Ancient history and vibrant daily life side by side.

TYRE (SOUR)

A UNESCO World Heritage Site known for its Roman ruins, ancient hippodrome, natural sandy beaches, and extraordinary underwater archaeological remains.

HARISSA

A hilltop pilgrimage site above the Bay of Jounieh, home to the statue of Our Lady of Lebanon and panoramic coastal views.

  1. Why is Lebanon called a cultural bridge between East and West?

    2. Lebanon blends Middle Eastern traditions with Western influences through its long history of trade and cultural exchange.

  2. Are Lebanese people friendly to tourists?

    3. Yes! Lebanese people are known for their hospitality and warmth, guests are often welcomed with food and friendly conversation.

QADISHA VALLEY

A UNESCO valley of sheer limestone cliffs, ancient monasteries, and mountain river trails. Lebanon's finest hiking destination.

  1. Is Lebanon only about history?

    2. Not at all! Lebanon is also known for its modern nightlife, beach resorts, mountain adventures, and vibrant café culture.

  2. What is dabke?

    3. Dabke is a traditional Lebanese folk dance performed at weddings and celebrations. It symbolizes unity, joy, and cultural identity.
05 — Importance of Eco-Tourism

Why It Matters

Eco-tourism is not simply one type of tourism among many in Lebanon, it is a direct investment in the country's natural future, a source of income for rural communities, and one of the most powerful arguments for protecting Lebanon's wild places from development and degradation.
01
Funding Conservation

Entrance and tour fees from Lebanon's reserves directly fund conservation staff and monitoring programs. Without eco-tourism income, many reserves would struggle to operate. Every visitor contributes to their survival.

02
Supporting Rural Communities

Eco-tourism creates livelihoods for rural communities as guides, hosts, and food producers, giving people a direct economic stake in protecting the natural environment around them.

03
Protecting Biodiversity

Lebanon's biodiversity faces threats from habitat loss, hunting, and pollution. Eco-tourism builds the economic case and public awareness needed to protect what remains.

04
Distributing Tourism Geographically

Most of Lebanon's tourism is concentrated in Beirut and coastal towns. Eco-tourism draws visitors to Akkar, the Chouf, and the Beqaa, spreading economic benefit more fairly and reducing pressure on overcrowded urban sites.

05
Building Environmental Awareness

Every person who hikes the Mountain Trail or watches a turtle nest returns home with a personal stake in Lebanon's natural world. Eco-tourism builds the conservation constituency Lebanon urgently needs.

06
A Sustainable Future

Lebanon's mountains, forests, and coastline cannot be exported or depleted. In a country facing repeated economic crises, eco-tourism is one of the most resilient and renewable assets available.
